How Private Foundations Work

Private foundations are nonprofit organizations funded by a single major source, whether that's an individual, a family, or a corporation. Unlike public charities that rely on broad public fundraising, private foundations typically receive their funding from one donor or a small group of related donors. They use this endowment to make grants that support charitable work across education, health, the arts, social services, and other program areas.

The IRS requires every private foundation to distribute at least 5% of its net investment assets each year as grants or qualifying distributions. This payout rule ensures that foundation assets are actively used to support charitable purposes rather than sitting idle. Foundations that fail to meet the minimum payout face excise taxes.

Critically for grant seekers, every private foundation must file IRS Form 990-PF annually. This public filing discloses the foundation's finances, officers, and every grant awarded during the year, including recipient names, amounts, and stated purposes. This transparency is what makes systematic foundation research possible. The United States has over 100,000 active private foundations, ranging from small family funds making a handful of grants to major institutions distributing hundreds of millions annually. If you're exploring grants for nonprofits, understanding how foundations operate is an essential first step.

Researching Foundation Giving Patterns

The 990-PF filing is the single most valuable resource for understanding a foundation's priorities. By reviewing several years of filings, you can identify which organizations a foundation has funded, what typical grant sizes look like, whether there's a geographic focus, and which program areas receive the most support. Patterns in past giving are the strongest predictor of future grants.

For example, a foundation that has consistently funded after-school programs in the Midwest at $25,000 to $50,000 per grant is unlikely to suddenly begin funding international health initiatives at $500,000. Giving histories reveal both the scope and the boundaries of a foundation's interests. FunderMatch analyzes 1.1M 990 filings to surface these patterns automatically, saving hours of manual research for organizations seeking grants for 501(c)(3) organizations.

Beyond individual grants, look at the overall trajectory: Is the foundation's giving increasing or decreasing? Are they expanding into new program areas? Have they added or dropped geographic regions? These trends tell you whether a foundation is a realistic prospect for your organization.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a private foundation?

A private foundation is a nonprofit organization funded by a single source — typically an individual, family, or corporation — that distributes grants to support charitable causes. Unlike public charities, private foundations primarily fund other organizations rather than running programs directly. They are classified as 501(c)(3) organizations and are subject to specific IRS regulations, including annual reporting requirements and minimum distribution rules.

How can I find a foundation's giving history?

Every private foundation is required to file IRS Form 990-PF annually, which lists all grants made during the year. These filings are public record and available through the IRS. FunderMatch indexes 7.5M grant records from these filings so you can search, filter, and analyze foundation giving data without reading through individual tax returns.

What is the 5% payout rule?

Private foundations are required by the IRS to distribute at least 5% of their net investment assets annually as grants or qualifying distributions. This payout requirement ensures that foundations actively support charitable work rather than simply accumulating wealth. Foundations that fail to meet this minimum distribution face excise taxes on the shortfall.

How do I know if a foundation funds my type of work?

The best indicator of a foundation's funding priorities is their past grants. Look at who they've funded, in what amounts, and in which program areas. Reviewing several years of 990-PF filings reveals consistent patterns in a foundation's giving. FunderMatch analyzes this automatically when you describe your mission, surfacing foundations whose giving history aligns with your work.
