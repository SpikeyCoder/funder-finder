Types of Grants Available for Nonprofits

Foundation grants for nonprofits generally fall into four categories, each serving a different organizational need. Understanding these distinctions helps you target the right funders and frame your ask effectively.

  • Program grants fund specific projects or initiatives, such as launching an after-school tutoring program or expanding a food distribution network. These are the most common type of foundation grant and typically require detailed project budgets and measurable outcomes.
  • Operating and general support grants cover core expenses like staff salaries, rent, insurance, and utilities. While harder to secure, these grants are the most flexible. According to the National Center for Charitable Statistics, general support represents roughly 20% of all foundation giving.
  • Capacity building grants strengthen your organization's infrastructure: technology upgrades, board development, strategic planning, or financial systems. These grants help nonprofits grow sustainably rather than just funding programs.
  • Capital and equipment grants fund major purchases such as vehicles, medical equipment, facility construction, or renovations. These are often larger awards with longer application timelines.

The U.S. foundation sector distributed over $105 billion in grants in recent years, with giving concentrated among the largest 5% of foundations. Smaller and mid-sized foundations, however, often have less competition and stronger community ties, making them valuable targets for nonprofit funding opportunities.

How to Find the Right Grant Funders

The biggest challenge in securing foundation grants is not the application itself but identifying which funders are genuinely aligned with your work. There are over 140,000 active grantmaking foundations in the United States, and most have specific giving priorities that are not always obvious from their names or websites.

Effective funder research starts with understanding a foundation's giving patterns: What NTEE codes (National Taxonomy of Exempt Entities) do their grantees fall under? What geographic regions do they prioritize? What is their typical grant size range? This information is publicly available through IRS 990-PF filings, but manually reviewing these documents across hundreds of potential funders is enormously time-consuming.

Mission alignment is the strongest predictor of grant success. A funder that has consistently supported organizations similar to yours in mission scope, budget size, and geographic focus is far more likely to fund your work than a large foundation where you are an outlier in their portfolio. Tools like free grant search engines can accelerate this matching process by analyzing giving histories at scale.

Why Mission-Based Grant Matching Works

Traditional grant research relies on keyword searches against foundation directories, which often surface irrelevant results or miss funders that use different terminology for similar work. AI-powered matching takes a fundamentally different approach: instead of matching keywords, it compares the semantic meaning of your mission against the actual giving patterns of foundations.

FunderMatch indexes 1.1 million IRS 990 filings and extracts structured data about each foundation's grantees, grant amounts, geographic distribution, and program areas. When you describe your nonprofit's mission, the system identifies funders whose historical giving aligns with your work, even if they use different language to describe similar focus areas.

This approach surfaces relevant funders that keyword searches miss entirely. A youth mentoring nonprofit, for example, might match with foundations categorized under education, workforce development, or community services, depending on how similar grantees were classified. Mission-based matching captures all of these connections automatically, saving hours of manual research per funder prospect.
